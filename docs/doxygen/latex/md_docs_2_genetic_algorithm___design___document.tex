This project employs a genetic algorithm to optimize circuit configurations. Genetic algorithms are heuristic search algorithms inspired by the process of natural selection. They use operations such as selection, crossover, and mutation to find the optimal solution. This document provides a detailed explanation of the design structure and optimization strategies implemented in the genetic algorithm.\hypertarget{md_docs_2_genetic_algorithm___design___document_autotoc_md38}{}\doxysection{\texorpdfstring{UML Graph}{UML Graph}}\label{md_docs_2_genetic_algorithm___design___document_autotoc_md38}

By preserving the best individuals, the elitism strategy ensures that the genetic algorithm does not lose the highest quality solutions found in each generation, thereby maintaining or improving the overall quality of the population over time.\hypertarget{md_docs_2_genetic_algorithm___design___document_autotoc_md57}{}\doxysubsection{\texorpdfstring{2. Selection Strategies}{2. Selection Strategies}}\label{md_docs_2_genetic_algorithm___design___document_autotoc_md57}
Selection strategies are critical in genetic algorithms as they determine how individuals are chosen to create the next generation. The implemented selection strategies in this project include Nary Tournament Selection, Rank Selection, and Roulette Wheel Selection. Each strategy has its advantages in maintaining diversity and preventing premature convergence.\hypertarget{md_docs_2_genetic_algorithm___design___document_autotoc_md58}{}\doxysubsubsection{\texorpdfstring{Nary Tournament Selection}{Nary Tournament Selection}}\label{md_docs_2_genetic_algorithm___design___document_autotoc_md58}
{\bfseries{Nary Tournament Selection}} is a method where \textquotesingle{}n\textquotesingle{} individuals are randomly chosen from the population, and the one with the highest fitness among them is selected as a parent. This process helps in maintaining a healthy diversity in the population and prevents premature convergence to suboptimal solutions.\hypertarget{md_docs_2_genetic_algorithm___design___document_autotoc_md59}{}\doxysubsubsection{\texorpdfstring{Rank Selection}{Rank Selection}}\label{md_docs_2_genetic_algorithm___design___document_autotoc_md59}
{\bfseries{Rank Selection}} assigns selection probabilities to individuals based on their rank rather than their raw fitness values. This ensures that even individuals with lower fitness have a chance of being selected, thereby maintaining diversity in the population.\hypertarget{md_docs_2_genetic_algorithm___design___document_autotoc_md60}{}\doxysubsubsection{\texorpdfstring{Roulette Wheel Selection}{Roulette Wheel Selection}}\label{md_docs_2_genetic_algorithm___design___document_autotoc_md60}
{\bfseries{Roulette Wheel Selection}} (also known as fitness proportionate selection) assigns selection probabilities proportional to the individuals\textquotesingle{} fitness values. Individuals with higher fitness have a higher chance of being selected, but there is still a probability for lower fitness individuals to be chosen, thus maintaining diversity.\hypertarget{md_docs_2_genetic_algorithm___design___document_autotoc_md61}{}\doxysubsection{\texorpdfstring{3. Crossover Strategies}{3. Crossover Strategies}}\label{md_docs_2_genetic_algorithm___design___document_autotoc_md61}
Crossover strategies play a vital role in genetic algorithms by combining the genetic information of parent individuals to create offspring for the next generation. This process helps in exploring the search space and finding better solutions.\hypertarget{md_docs_2_genetic_algorithm___design___document_autotoc_md62}{}\doxysubsubsection{\texorpdfstring{Pure\+Single\+Point\+Crossover}{Pure\+Single\+Point\+Crossover}}\label{md_docs_2_genetic_algorithm___design___document_autotoc_md62}
{\bfseries{\doxylink{class_pure_single_point_crossover}{Pure\+Single\+Point\+Crossover}}} involves selecting a random crossover point and exchanging the subsequences after this point between two parent individuals. This strategy helps to combine different features from the parents, potentially leading to better offspring.\hypertarget{md_docs_2_genetic_algorithm___design___document_autotoc_md63}{}\doxysubsubsection{\texorpdfstring{Two\+Point\+Crossover}{Two\+Point\+Crossover}}\label{md_docs_2_genetic_algorithm___design___document_autotoc_md63}
{\bfseries{\doxylink{class_two_point_crossover}{Two\+Point\+Crossover}}} is similar to the single-\/point crossover but involves selecting two random points. The segments between these two points are exchanged between the parents, leading to potentially more diverse offspring.\hypertarget{md_docs_2_genetic_algorithm___design___document_autotoc_md64}{}\doxysubsubsection{\texorpdfstring{Uniform\+Crossover}{Uniform\+Crossover}}\label{md_docs_2_genetic_algorithm___design___document_autotoc_md64}
{\bfseries{\doxylink{class_uniform_crossover}{Uniform\+Crossover}}} randomly selects genes from each parent, independent of their positions, resulting in a higher level of genetic diversity in the offspring. Simulated Annealing (SA) is a probabilistic technique used for approximating the global optimum of a given function. In the context of genetic algorithms, SA is utilized to accept or reject new individuals (offspring) based on a temperature-\/controlled probability.
